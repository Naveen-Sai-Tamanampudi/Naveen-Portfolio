Study of Flight Schedules

I conducted an in-depth analysis of U.S. flight departures, arrivals, cancellations, delays, and diversions from 2019 to 2023 using a 100,000-row sample from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics. By preprocessing and visualizing this complex dataset, I uncovered key trends in airport and airline performance, delay causes, and the impacts of major events like COVID-19. My work provides actionable insights to support better airline scheduling, airport expansion, and operational improvements

Key Insights:
• Chicago was the busiest airport with 5,035 departures, closely followed by Atlanta (4,994) and Dallas/Fort Worth (4,283).
• Chicago also had the most cancellations (163), followed by Dallas/Fort Worth (133) and New York. However, the highest cancellation rates were at smaller airports like Adak Island (50%), Cheyenne (33%), and Joplin (25%). The most common cancellation type was Type-B, accounting for 35.44% of all cancellations.
• 2019 saw the highest number of flights (24,549), dropping to 14,801 in 2020 due to COVID-19, then rising again in 2021 and 2022. Average departure delay was 10.09 minutes and average arrival delay was 4.35 minutes, with delays dropping sharply in 2020 and rising again by 2023 (departure delay: 11.9 minutes; arrival delay: 6.86 minutes). Carrier-related delays were the most common, responsible for 34.4% of all delays.
• SouthWest Airlines operated the most flights and had the highest number of cancellations (654). JetBlue Airways caused the highest average delays (departure: 17 min, arrival: 11.61 min), while SkyWest Airlines had the most diversions (32). Only three airlines-Delta, Republic, and Endeavour-achieved negative average arrival delays (i.e., flights arriving early).
